## Core Gameplay Mechanics

### Primary Mechanics
<!-- 
List and describe the core gameplay mechanics players will engage with. 
Explain how they contribute to the game's challenge and fun. 
-->
- Supply chain building: Players expand territory by placing tokens in rooms adjacent to their existing chain. Every room in the path from starting corner to Boss Chamber must hold at least one token, the path must be more than 7 rooms long not including the boss room. Cannot go diagonal. 
- Character movement: Each player's figure can only move through rooms already in their chain. The figure must physically reach the Boss Chamber to win.
- Intransitive faction bonuses: Factions beat each other in a cycle (Elf beats Orc, Orc beats Angel, Angel beats Demon, Demon beats Elf) The advantaged faction gets +1 toward majority in contested rooms.
- Ability cards: Three card types (Remove, Swap, Skip) let players disrupt opponent chains and protect their own.
- Special rooms: Portal Rooms let players teleport their figure anywhere in their chain. Trap Rooms force any figure that enters to skip their next turn.

### Goals and Challenges
<!-- 
Detail what players aim to achieve and the obstacles they must overcome. 
Explain how these challenges drive player engagement. 
-->
- Build an unbroken token chain from your starting corner to the Boss Chamber, containting 7+ rooms, with no diagonals. 
- Hold majority tokens in the Boss Chamber
- Have your character figure present in the Boss Chamber
- All three conditions must be true simultaneously at the end of a full round from your turn

### Progression
<!-- 
Describe how the gameplay evolves over time. 
Are there new abilities, unlockable levels, skill trees, or difficulty scaling? 
-->
- Early game focuses on expanding chains outward from corners. Midgame becomes contested as chains intersect and ability cards start disrupting supply lines. Endgame converges on the Boss Chamber where majority control can flip multiple times before someone holds all three win conditions through a full round.

### Game Rules
<!-- 
Outline the core rules governing the gameplay experience. 
Ensure they are clear, structured, and intuitive. 
-->
- Each player starts with their character figure and 2 tokens in their starting corner. Draw no cards to start.
- On your turn, either play an ability card, or roll a D6 and resolve the matching action:
-   1: place 1 token
-   2: place 2 tokens
-   3: place 3 tokens
-   4: move your piece 1 room
-   5: move up to 2 rooms
-   6: draw an ability card! (if you have 3, have to discard a current one, only have up to 3 ability cards at a time) 
  
- A room is contested when it holds tokens from more than one faction. Most tokens controls it. In a tie, no one controls it.
- The faction with the intransitive advantage gets +1 toward majority in contested rooms.
- Tokens can only be removed by ability cards, not through normal play.
- Win check happens when 1 player holds true all winning conditions after a full round of their turn.

## Unique Selling Points (USP)
<!-- 
Identify what makes your game stand out from others in its genre. 
Highlight key features that differentiate it in the market. 
-->
- No combat, conflict is entirelly by cotornally territories, insetad of eliminating opponents.
- Easier than Risk or Catan
- Three-part win condition: Requiring a supply chain, majority, and physical presence means no single strategy dominates.
- Intransitive faction system: Rock-paper-scissors-style faction cycle adds asymmetry with minimal complexity. One number (+1) and a four-step cycle.
- Plays in under an hour: Sits between lightweight party games and heavy strategy games.
- No economy: removed currency system to keep cognitive load low and turns moving quickly.

## Inspiration

### Sources
<!-- 
List books, movies, historical events, or games that influenced this project. 
-->
- Catan — territory expansion and competitive tension in multiplayer
- Risk — strategic territory control and the danger of overextending lines
- Splatoon — spreading colors across a map to claim territory, winning by coverage rather than combat
- Rock-paper-scissors — intransitive relationships as a structure for balanced asymmetry
- Ludo — the starting corner mechanic, where each player begins from their own home base and races toward the center

### Why It Matters
<!-- 
Explain how these inspirations shape the game’s mechanics, visuals, or themes. 
-->
- Catan and Risk established that territory control creates natural tension without direct combat. Splatoon is the most direct visual inspiration with color spreading across the board room by room, with the board itself showing who's winning at a glance. Ludo contributed the fundamental layout: four players, four corners, one shared destination in the center. Rock-paper-scissors provided the mathematical backbone for the faction system with each one balancing each other out.

### Challenges and Considerations

#### Potential Risks
<!-- 
Identify elements that could fail or require refinement. 
Examples: balancing issues, unclear mechanics, technological constraints. 
-->
- Balance: The intransitive cycle is theoretically even, but with 4 players a faction disadvantaged by two opponents may struggle. Needs playtesting across varied faction combinations.
- Endgame pacing: The one-round delay on the win condition prevents sudden victories but could drag if disruption is too easy. The 20-round tiebreaker is a fallback.
- Special room placement: Portal Rooms too close to the Boss Chamber could make certain paths dominant. Trap Rooms placed off natural crossing routes may be ignored entirely.

#### Feasibility
<!-- 
Describe any technological, financial, or development constraints. 
How will you mitigate these risks? 
-->
- All components can be assembled from craft materials for under $20
- Main thing is needing to playtesting time to calibrate win condition timing and faction balance

## Pitch Preparation

<!-- 
Provide a concise and engaging summary of the game concept and theme.

- Encroach is a 2–4 player territory control board game for ages 12 and up, playable in 30–60 minutes. ## Appendix
<!-- 
Include any additional sketches, mood boards, or early design mockups if available.  
If digital assets are unavailable, describe any rough concepts you have in mind. 
-->
- Encroach went through three major design choices to make it better/simpler. (and changed its name) 
- The original version included a coin economy, six ability card types, four special room types, and a single D6 action system.
- The economy was removed, ability cards reduced to three types, special rooms reduced to two types, and the dice system replaced with 2D6 for better probability distribution.
- The final design retains the original core — supply chain territory control, four asymmetric factions, and a three-part win condition — while removing every mechanic that didn't directly serve that core.
